Why This Exists

Finance should help you,
not confuse you.

Most people feel overwhelmed by financial decisions — not because finance is hard, but because it is deliberately made complicated. We are here to change that.

01

Too much noise, not enough signal

Every day there are hundreds of opinions on what to buy, sell, or avoid. Most of it is noise. Real financial planning is quiet, disciplined, and deeply personal.

02

Goals before investments

Nobody needs a mutual fund. People need a house, a child's education, a peaceful retirement. We start with what you want, and work backwards with maths — not products.

03

Honesty as the foundation

No fake projections. No misaligned incentives. No jargon to keep you dependent. Everything here is designed to help you make better decisions for yourself.

04

Time is your most powerful asset

The difference between starting at 25 vs 35 is not one decade — it is a lifetime of wealth. Understanding this changes everything about how you think about money today.

05

Protection before growth

A single medical emergency without insurance can undo years of careful saving. Building a safety net is not optional — it is the very first step in any financial plan.

06

Personalised, not generic

A 28-year-old software engineer in Chandigarh and a 45-year-old businessperson in Mumbai do not have the same financial plan. Context matters. Nuance matters.

Financial Education

A roadmap through every stage of life.

Financial planning is not a product or a portfolio. It is a series of decisions — made consistently, over decades — that determine whether money becomes your servant or your master.

Your 20s — Age 21 to 30
Build the Foundation

Your 20s are not the time to get rich — they are the time to avoid getting poor. Build habits that compound just as powerfully as money does.

  • Build an emergency fund covering 6 months of expenses before investing anything
  • Get a term life insurance policy if you have dependants — the younger you buy, the cheaper it is for life
  • Get health insurance independent of your employer; corporate cover ends when your job does
  • Start any SIP, even ₹1,000/month — the habit matters more than the amount at this stage
  • Avoid EMIs on depreciating assets (phones, gadgets, holidays)
  • Understand your payslip — taxable income, PF deductions, HRA — before spending
The single most important financial truth: Time in the market beats timing the market. A ₹5,000 SIP started at 22 becomes approximately ₹3.5 crores at 60 at 12% CAGR. Started at 32, it becomes ₹1.1 crores. One decade costs ₹2.4 crores.
Your 30s — Age 30 to 40
Accelerate and Align

Your income is growing. Your responsibilities are growing too. This decade is about channelling that income growth into long-term wealth before lifestyle inflation swallows it.

  • Increase your SIP by at least 10% every year — preferably in April, after your salary increment
  • Review your term insurance cover — it should be at least 10–15× your annual income
  • Separate your financial goals clearly: child's education (15-year horizon), home down payment (5-year), retirement (25-year)
  • Invest based on goal timelines, not emotions or tips
  • Avoid surrendering or pausing SIPs during market downturns — that is precisely the wrong time
  • Pay off any high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans) aggressively before investing
  • Start a Will — it is not morbid, it is responsible
On home loans: A home is not an investment — it is a consumption asset. Do not over-borrow. Keep your home loan EMI below 35% of your take-home pay, and maintain your investment SIPs even as you repay the loan.
Your 40s — Age 40 to 50
Consolidate and Protect

By your 40s, you should have meaningful savings. The priority shifts from accumulation to protection and smarter allocation.

  • Begin gradually reducing equity exposure by 2–3% per year — a simple rule: equity % ≈ 110 minus your age
  • Review your insurance cover — your income, liabilities, and dependants have all changed since you first bought policies
  • Top up your health insurance with a super top-up plan — medical costs at 60 can be enormous
  • Do a comprehensive net worth review annually — assets vs liabilities, progress toward goals
  • Maximise NPS contributions for the tax benefit under Section 80CCD(1B)
  • Avoid high-risk investments at this stage — you have less time to recover from losses
The biggest financial mistake of the 40s: Stopping SIPs to pay for a child's education or a wedding. These goals should have been separately funded for years. If they were not, a personal loan is preferable to breaking your retirement investment.
Your 50s and Beyond — Age 50+
Transition to Freedom

The finishing straight. The quality of your financial life from 60 onwards is almost entirely determined by decisions made in your 30s and 40s. But there is still meaningful work to do.

  • Begin building a 2–3 year cash/liquid buffer — so you never need to sell equity in a downturn at 62
  • Plan your SWP (Systematic Withdrawal Plan) strategy — how you will draw from your corpus in retirement
  • Review your Will and nominations across all financial accounts and policies
  • Understand the tax implications of each withdrawal source — EPF, NPS, mutual funds, FDs differ significantly
  • Consider Senior Citizen Savings Scheme (SCSS) and PM Vaya Vandana Yojana for guaranteed income
  • Do not stop all equity — even in retirement, a 20–30% equity allocation helps the corpus outlast you
Longevity risk is real: Plan for your corpus to last 25–30 years in retirement. At 7% inflation, expenses double every 10 years. A ₹50,000/month expense today becomes ₹1.85 lakh/month in 20 years. Plan accordingly.
Always — At Every Stage
The Principles That Never Change
  • Spend less than you earn. The oldest rule in finance, and the most violated.
  • Insurance is not an investment. Buy term life and health insurance for protection. Invest separately for growth.
  • Inflation is the silent tax. Keeping money idle in a savings account loses real value every year.
  • Diversification is not optional. No single asset, city, sector, or currency should represent your entire financial life.
  • Debt is a tool, not a lifestyle. Borrow only for assets that appreciate or generate income.
  • Review annually, not daily. Checking your portfolio every day is not investing — it is gambling with your emotions.

How IRR works for insurance IRR treats every premium paid as a negative cashflow (outflow) and every benefit received as a positive cashflow (inflow). The IRR is the discount rate at which NPV = 0. This is exactly how Excel's IRR() function works.
